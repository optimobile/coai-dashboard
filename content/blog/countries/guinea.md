# AI Safety Framework for Guinea: Building Tomorrow's AI Governance Today

## Executive Summary

As the world stands on the precipice of a new technological era, the Republic of Guinea has a unique opportunity to establish itself as a leader in responsible artificial intelligence (AI) innovation. With the European Union's AI Act set to take effect on February 2, 2026, the urgency for Guinea to implement a comprehensive AI safety framework has never been more critical. The Act will have extraterritorial reach, impacting any entity that offers AI systems or services to EU citizens, regardless of where the entity is based. For Guinea to competitively engage with the global digital economy and attract foreign investment, aligning with international best practices in AI governance is not just an option, but a necessity. A proactive approach to AI safety will not only mitigate potential risks but also unlock immense opportunities for economic growth, social development, and the modernization of public services. This is the moment for Guinea to build a future where AI is harnessed safely, ethically, and for the benefit of all its citizens.

## The Current State of AI in Guinea

Guinea is at a pivotal juncture in its digital transformation journey. The government has demonstrated a clear commitment to embracing technology as a catalyst for progress, with the validation of a national AI diagnostic report and a 10-year AI roadmap for 2026-2035 [1]. This forward-thinking strategy aims to leverage AI as a strategic driver for development and to strengthen the nation's digital sovereignty. While AI adoption is still in its nascent stages, the potential for impact across key sectors of the Guinean economy is substantial. The mining industry, a cornerstone of the nation's economy, could utilize AI for optimizing exploration and extraction processes, enhancing safety, and improving environmental management. In agriculture, which employs a significant portion of the population, AI-powered solutions can boost crop yields, predict weather patterns, and improve supply chain efficiency. The government's digital transformation initiatives, including the development of a Tier III national data center and the expansion of fiber optic networks, are laying the foundational infrastructure for a thriving digital ecosystem [2]. Furthermore, Guinea has already taken a crucial step in establishing a regulatory framework for the digital age with the enactment of the Law on Cybersecurity and Personal Data Protection in 2016 [3]. This existing legislation provides a solid starting point for the development of a more specific and comprehensive AI governance framework.

## The AI Governance Gap

Despite these positive developments, Guinea is one of 118 countries that currently lack a formal AI governance framework. This gap presents significant risks that could undermine the nation's progress and expose its citizens and businesses to a range of harms. Without clear regulations and oversight, the deployment of AI systems could lead to algorithmic bias, job displacement, and the erosion of privacy. The absence of a national AI strategy can also stifle innovation, as businesses may be hesitant to invest in AI technologies without legal and ethical guardrails. For Guinean citizens, the lack of AI governance could result in a lack of transparency and accountability in how their data is used, and a diminished ability to seek redress for AI-related harms. For businesses, it creates an uncertain operating environment and could hinder their ability to compete in the global marketplace, where compliance with regulations like the EU AI Act is increasingly becoming a prerequisite for doing business. Closing this governance gap is therefore a critical priority for Guinea to ensure that the development and deployment of AI is aligned with its national values and the best interests of its people.

## Implementation Roadmap

CSOAI proposes a phased approach to the implementation of the AI Safety Framework in Guinea, designed to deliver tangible results in a structured and efficient manner:

*   **Phase 1: Assessment and Planning (3 months):** This initial phase will involve a comprehensive assessment of Guinea's existing legal and regulatory landscape, as well as its technological infrastructure and human capital. In close collaboration with government stakeholders, we will develop a detailed implementation plan and customize the white-label framework to align with Guinea's national priorities.
*   **Phase 2: Framework Deployment (6 months):** In this phase, the AI Safety Framework will be officially launched and integrated into Guinea's legal and regulatory system. This will involve the establishment of the 33-Agent Byzantine Council for AI and the dissemination of the new policies and standards to all relevant stakeholders.
*   **Phase 3: Training 1,000 AI Safety Analysts (12 months):** To build local capacity, CSOAI will launch a nationwide training program to certify 1,000 Guinean professionals as CEASAI AI Safety Analysts. This will create a skilled workforce to support the implementation and enforcement of the AI framework.
*   **Phase 4: Full Operational Capability (Ongoing):** In this final phase, the AI Safety Framework will be fully operational, with the Byzantine Council providing ongoing oversight and the certified AI Safety Analysts conducting regular audits and risk assessments. The SOAI-PDCA methodology will ensure that the framework remains up-to-date and effective in the long term.
