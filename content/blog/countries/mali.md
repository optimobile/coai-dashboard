# AI Safety Framework for Mali: Building Tomorrow's AI Governance Today

## Executive Summary

As the world stands on the precipice of a new technological era, the Republic of Mali has a unique and time-sensitive opportunity to establish itself as a leader in responsible AI adoption. With the European Union's AI Act set to take effect on February 2, 2026, the urgency for a comprehensive AI safety framework in Mali has never been greater. The nation's burgeoning digital ecosystem, coupled with a young and dynamic population, creates a fertile ground for AI-driven innovation. However, without a robust governance structure, Mali risks falling behind, exposing its citizens and businesses to the multifaceted risks of unregulated AI. This is not merely a matter of compliance but a strategic imperative to unlock the full potential of AI for economic growth, social development, and global competitiveness. The Council of Safe AI (CSOAI) presents a turnkey solution to bridge this gap, offering a clear and actionable roadmap for Mali to build a secure and prosperous digital future.

## Current State of AI in Mali

Mali's journey into the digital age is marked by a growing, albeit nascent, adoption of artificial intelligence. The most prominent applications of AI in the country are concentrated in the education and telecommunications sectors. A groundbreaking initiative by RobotsMali, a local non-profit, is leveraging AI to generate illustrated children's books in Bambara, one of Mali's national languages. This project not only addresses the scarcity of local language educational materials but also demonstrates the potential of AI to preserve and promote cultural heritage. The use of tools like ChatGPT and Google Translate for content creation and translation is a testament to the ingenuity of Malian innovators in adapting global technologies to local needs.

The Malian government has also embarked on a series of digital transformation initiatives aimed at modernizing public services. The recent launch of platforms for biometric driver's licenses and electronic toll systems indicates a commitment to leveraging technology for improved efficiency and security. Furthermore, the partnership between the International Finance Corporation (IFC) and Orange Mali to expand digital connectivity, particularly in rural areas, is laying the critical infrastructure for wider AI adoption. These initiatives, while not explicitly AI-focused, are creating the enabling environment for AI to flourish. However, the absence of a dedicated national AI strategy or specific technology regulations presents a significant governance gap that needs to be addressed to ensure that the benefits of AI are maximized and the risks are mitigated.

## The AI Governance Gap

Mali is one of 118 countries that have yet to establish a formal AI governance framework. This absence of a regulatory structure creates a vacuum, leaving the nation vulnerable to a range of risks. For citizens, the lack of oversight can lead to the misuse of personal data, algorithmic bias that perpetuates social inequalities, and a general lack of transparency and accountability in AI systems. For businesses, the uncertainty of the regulatory landscape can stifle innovation, deter investment, and create legal and reputational risks. The impending EU AI Act further complicates the situation, as Malian companies that do business with the EU will be required to comply with its stringent regulations. Without a national framework, Malian businesses will be at a competitive disadvantage, struggling to navigate the complex and costly process of compliance on their own.

The AI governance gap is not just a matter of risk mitigation; it is also a missed opportunity. A well-defined framework can foster trust in AI, encourage responsible innovation, and attract foreign investment. It can also provide a clear roadmap for the development of a skilled AI workforce, ensuring that Malians are not just consumers of AI but also creators and beneficiaries. By failing to act, Mali risks becoming a passive recipient of AI technologies developed elsewhere, with little control over their impact on its society and economy.

## CSOAI's Solution for Mali

The Council of Safe AI (CSOAI) offers a comprehensive, white-label AI Safety Framework designed to be adapted and implemented by national governments. Our solution is not a one-size-fits-all approach but a flexible and customizable framework that can be tailored to Mali's specific needs and priorities. The framework is built on the principles of transparency, accountability, and human-centricity, and is designed to be fully compliant with international standards, including the EU AI Act.

At the heart of our solution is the CEASAI (Certified European AI Safety Analyst) certification program. This program is designed to create a new generation of AI safety professionals in Mali, equipped with the skills and knowledge to assess, audit, and manage AI systems. The CEASAI certification is a globally recognized qualification that will open up new career opportunities for Malian citizens and create a local talent pool to support the growth of the AI industry.

To ensure robust and independent oversight, our framework includes the establishment of a 33-Agent Byzantine Council for AI. This council, composed of a diverse group of stakeholders including government officials, industry experts, academics, and civil society representatives, will be responsible for overseeing the implementation of the AI safety framework and ensuring its ongoing effectiveness. The council will be supported by our proprietary SOAI-PDCA (Plan-Do-Check-Act) compliance methodology, a continuous improvement model that ensures that the framework remains agile and responsive to the evolving AI landscape.

## Implementation Roadmap

CSOAI has developed a four-phase implementation roadmap to guide Mali on its journey to full AI governance:

*   **Phase 1: Assessment and Planning (3 months):** This initial phase involves a comprehensive assessment of Mali's existing digital infrastructure, legal and regulatory landscape, and AI readiness. This will be followed by a collaborative planning process to tailor the AI Safety Framework to Mali's specific context.
*   **Phase 2: Framework Deployment (6 months):** In this phase, the customized framework will be deployed, including the establishment of the 33-Agent Byzantine Council and the integration of the SOAI-PDCA compliance methodology into government and industry workflows.
*   **Phase 3: Training 1,000 AI Safety Analysts (12 months):** This phase will focus on the large-scale training and certification of 1,000 Malian citizens as CEASAI certified AI Safety Analysts. This will be achieved through a combination of online and in-person training programs, delivered in partnership with local educational institutions.
*   **Phase 4: Full Operational Capability (Ongoing):** The final phase marks the achievement of full operational capability, with the AI Safety Framework fully integrated into the national ecosystem. This phase will also involve ongoing monitoring, evaluation, and refinement of the framework to ensure its long-term effectiveness.
